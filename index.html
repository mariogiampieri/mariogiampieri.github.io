Next Generation American Suburbs

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Two Sites, Two Scales, Two Strategies

Traditionally, new development along the outer edges of metropolitan areas produces car-based, low density suburbs with repetitive single-family houses resting on uniform lots. This development pattern traditionally increases the burden on existing municipal infrastructure and requires the construction of costly transportation infrastructure and other utilities to support population growth. Various sources speculate that the introduction of autonomous electric vehicles (AEVs) into the consumer market will exacerbate these trends by allowing for even more dispersed suburban development.

This project takes two distinct suburban communities within the “Texas Triangle Megaregion” as key references due to their aggressive rate of suburban growth, and their strategic locations within the context of autonomous implementation (limited topographical variation, mild climate, and powerful regional economy).

The project seeks to frame how the design of the built environment, considered in tandem with emergent AEV technology, can lead to the creation of higher performing suburban environments within the contexts of transportation access, environmental justice, and equitable development.

The project addresses three primary scales of planning and design:

  1. The Metro scale
  2. The District scale
  3. The Block scale
The Texas Triangle (also known as Texaplex) is a region of Texas which contains the state’s five largest cities and is home to the majority of the state’s population.
  1. Dallas-Fort Worth-Arlington
  2. San Antonio - New Braunfels
  3. Houston -Sugar Land-Baytown
  4. College Station
  5. Austin -Round Rock-San Marcos

Estate Residential

Existing Conditions

Ultra-low density single family districts are typically located far away from regional commercial areas, employment campuses, and regional parks which makes them highly dependent on excessive daily vehicular trips. These large lots are typically landscaped with lawn, which, due to it’s high water and maintenance requirements, is a carbon and energy intensive material, adding to the already high consumption footprints of these land uses. In the future, these areas offer opportunities for rethinking the role of shared transportation, and the co-location of local remote office space.

Multi-Family Residential

Existing Conditions

Multi-family housing makes up less than 14 percent of all residential land uses in the US today. Nevertheless, there is a significant demand for more affordable housing in general - especially in the suburbs where high-end development has dominated the market since the great recession. Today’s multifamily residential areas typically feature 2 parking spaces per unit, and often lack meaningful space for recreation in spite of the public health values that access to open space can provide. As cities across the country struggle to update zoning codes to include more “missing middle” housing, from town homes to higher-density models, this category is a major innovation opportunity area.

Neighborhood Commercial

Existing Conditions

Commercial areas in the suburban context are typified by either big box regional retail centers or smaller-scale neighborhood shopping plazas. While the former is notorious for its excessive parking and carbon intensive waste streams, the latter is typically more distributed and offers more specialized services. Nevertheless, as the future of retail continues to trend toward online shopping, these strategically located brick and mortar business areas offer a variety of significant retrofit opportunities. Their proximity to residential neighborhoods positions them to play a key role in districtscale pedestrianization and micro-mobility services, and serve as local AEV dispatch and charging centers.

Professional Campus

Existing Conditions

The office park has been a standard feature in the American suburbs since the 1950s, and in terms of typical zoning and land use, there has been very little innovation since the post WWII era. With nearly 100% of professional campus employees communizing via private vehicle, these spaces are typically filled by an overwhelming percentage of on-site parking. Building footprints are typically large, which makes them hard to adapt to new uses or shifting real estate markets, and lanscaped area is often composed of hardscape, lawn and other low environmental performance materials.
